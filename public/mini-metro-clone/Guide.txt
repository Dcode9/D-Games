The Algorithmic Metropolis: A Technical Deconstruction of Mini Metro1. Architectural Overview and System PhilosophyThe design of Mini Metro, developed by Dinosaur Polo Club, represents a masterclass in minimalist simulation theory. Unlike traditional transport simulations that rely on granular, high-fidelity modeling of individual agents with complex internal desires, Mini Metro achieves depth through the emergent behavior of simple systems interacting within strict topological constraints. For a systems architect or a developer tasked with reconstructing this logic—specifically for the purpose of guiding an AI coding assistant like GitHub Copilot—it is insufficient to merely understand the surface-level rules. One must understand the underlying mathematical graph, the stochastic "Director" that governs procedural generation, and the agent-based logic that creates the illusion of a living city.This report serves as the definitive technical specification and logic rulebook for the simulation. It deconstructs the game into its constituent atoms: the graph-theoretical model of the network, the stochastic processes of city growth, the agent-based logic of passengers, and the procedural geometry of the rendering pipeline. The objective is to provide a logic prompt so detailed that an AI could reconstruct the game’s core loop without hallucinating mechanics.1.1 The Philosophy of Elegant ConstraintThe central tension of the simulation is "Complexity via Constraint." The player does not control the city; the city is an autonomous, procedural adversary that grows according to a "Scheduling" algorithm. The player controls only the response to this growth: the topology of the transit network. The simulation is a continuous optimization problem where the solution space (the metro network) is mutable, but the problem space (the city and its passengers) is immutable and exponentially expanding.The simulation operates on two distinct layers:The Logical Layer: A directed graph where nodes (stations) are connected by weighted edges (tracks). This layer handles pathfinding, capacity, and scoring.The Visual Layer: A procedural geometry renderer that interprets the Logical Layer into smooth Catmull-Rom splines, dynamic meshes, and audio-reactive animations.Recreating the game requires a disciplined separation of these layers. The "Model" must be deterministic and grid-based, while the "View" must be fluid and analog.2. Procedural City Generation AlgorithmsThe spawning of stations appears random to the player, but it follows a sophisticated "Scheduling" algorithm designed to create specific topographical challenges. This system acts as a "Director AI," managing the difficulty curve by controlling the distribution of station types.3.1 The "Director" AI and Scheduling SystemThe game utilizes a pattern similar to Left 4 Dead's AI Director, but for city planning. It does not roll a purely random die for every spawn; instead, it draws from pre-defined "buckets" or schedules to ensure a playable but challenging distribution.The Logic of Schedules:
Research indicates that spawning is controlled by a system called "Scheduling".Pre-defined Patterns: There are multiple spawn schedules for a map (e.g., 5-10 different patterns).Deterministic Randomness: The game selects a schedule at random. A schedule defines a sequence of station types to spawn over time (e.g., CCCTTCC - Circle, Circle, Circle, Triangle, Triangle, Circle, Circle).Cooldowns: After a schedule is exhausted, there is a cooldown period (0+ days) before a new random schedule is selected.Implication: This prevents "impossible" scenarios where the RNG might otherwise spawn 50 Squares in a row. It ensures the ratio of Circles to Triangles remains manageable while still introducing variance.3.2 Zoning and City AreasThe map is not a single homogeneous zone. It is divided into "City Areas" or clusters.Area Definition: An area is likely defined by a centroid and a radius, or a polygon.Ratio Control: Each area has specific parameters:PermittedTypes: Which shapes can spawn here (e.g., "Residential" zones might only spawn Circles).StartDay: The game week when this area becomes active.Density: The minimum required spacing between stations in this area.MaxCount: Hard limits on specific shapes (e.g., "Max 1 Square per zone").Logic Flow for Station Spawning:Trigger: The SpawnTimer hits zero.Select Area: The Director picks a valid City Area based on current StartDay and Density.Select Type: The Director pulls the next shape from the active Schedule.Positioning: A random point is chosen within the Area.Validation: The system checks Vector2.Distance(NewPoint, ExistingStation) > MinSeparation.If invalid, retry $N$ times. If still invalid, select a different Area.Instantiate: The station is created, and the SpawnTimer is reset based on the current difficulty curve.3.3 Dynamic Station ConversionA critical mechanic for disrupting player optimization is the "Station Conversion."Mechanism: A station that is already on the map (usually a Circle) spontaneously transforms into a rarer type (Triangle, Square, or Special).Trigger: This is a hard-coded algorithmic event, separate from the standard spawn schedule. It is often triggered by week count or passenger throughput thresholds.Strategic Impact: This forces the player to re-route lines, as a line that was previously optimized for Circles may now be inefficient for the new Square.3.4 Map Expansion Mechanics (Zoom)As stations spawn closer to the screen edges, the camera must zoom out to maintain a playable margin. This is not a discrete "step" but a continuous floating-point interpolation.Algorithm for Camera Expansion:Calculate Bounds: Every frame (or every spawn event), compute the Axis-Aligned Bounding Box (AABB) encapsulated by all active Station.Position vectors.Apply Margin: Add a padding buffer (e.g., 10-20% of screen width) to this AABB.Target Size: Calculate the required Orthographic Size to fit this padded AABB.Damping: Use a SmoothDamp or Lerp function to interpolate the CurrentCameraSize toward TargetCameraSize.Note: The zoom logic likely ignores temporary UI elements and focuses solely on the graph nodes.4. Network Topology: Graph Theory ImplementationThe fundamental data structure of Mini Metro is a dynamic, user-mutable directed graph. Unlike standard graph problems where edges are static, the edges here are the primary variable controlled by the user.4.1 Node Definitions (The Station Entity)Nodes in the graph represent Stations. To support the simulation, a Station entity must encapsulate the following data:Unique ID: For serialization and debugging.Position: Vector2 (World Space).Type: Enum { Circle, Triangle, Square, Special }.Passengers: A Queue (FIFO) of Passenger agents waiting at this node.Capacity: Integer limit.Standard: 6 passengers.Interchange: 18 passengers.connectedLines: A list of Line objects that currently service this station.4.2 Edge Definitions (The Tracks)Edges represent the physical track between two stations. An Edge is defined by:Node A / Node B: The stations it connects.Line ID: The metro line (color) this edge belongs to.Length: The Euclidean distance (or Spline Arc Length) between nodes.Directionality: While tracks appear bidirectional, the graph logic treats connectivity as undirected (passengers can travel A to B or B to A), but individual trains traverse them in a specific direction at any given moment.4.3 The Line Object and "Ghost" LogicA "Line" is a higher-order collection of Edges.Data Structure: A Line is an ordered List<Station>.Constraint: A Line cannot branch. It is a single continuous path or a cycle.Loop Detection: If Line.FirstStation == Line.LastStation, the line is a Loop. This radically alters train physics (no need to reverse direction/stop at terminals).The Ghost Line Mechanic:
When a player drags a line to extend it, the system calculates a "Ghost Line".State: InputState.Dragging.Logic: A temporary edge is drawn from the LastStation to the MousePosition.Snapping: A spatial query checks if MousePosition is within the CatchmentRadius of any valid Station.If yes, the Ghost Line snaps to that Station.If no, it follows the cursor.Commit: On MouseUp, if the Ghost Line is snapped, the Station is appended to the Line list, and the Graph is marked "Dirty" for pathfinding recalculation.4.4 Dynamic Graph RemappingWhen a player modifies a line, the graph topology changes instantly.Event: OnLineChanged.Impact:Stranded Passengers: Passengers currently on a train that is on a deleted segment are dumped at the next station.Pathfinding Invalidated: All waiting passengers must clear their cached paths and request a new path from the Pathfinder based on the new topology.Time Slicing: To prevent frame drops on mobile devices, this recalculation is likely spread across multiple frames.5. Agent Simulation: Passenger Logic and PathfindingThe most critical logic in the game is how passengers choose their routes. They are intelligent agents interacting with the graph topology. The system employs a "Time-Weighted" pathfinding algorithm rather than a simple distance check.5.1 Passenger PropertiesA Passenger is a lightweight object or struct:SpawnTime: Timestamp of creation (for sorting/priority).Origin: The station where they spawned.Destination Type: The shape they want to reach (e.g., Triangle). Crucially, they do not have a specific destination station, only a destination type. They will accept any station of that type.Logic Rule: A passenger never wants to go to a station of the same type they are currently at. Demand is strictly heterogeneous.5.2 The Pathfinding Algorithm (Modified A*)The game uses a directed graph search. Since there are multiple valid destination nodes (e.g., any of the 5 Triangle stations), the algorithm is a "One-to-Many" search, often optimized to find the "Best" candidate.Cost Function $f(n) = g(n) + h(n)$
Research suggests the cost function is nuanced :$g(n)$ (Actual Cost):Travel Time: $\frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{TrainSpeed}}$.Wait Time: The estimated time until a train arrives. This is derived from the line's frequency ($\frac{\text{Loop Duration}}{\text{Train Count}}$).Transfer Penalty: A heavy static cost added when switching lines (e.g., equivalent to traversing a large distance). This discourages passengers from making redundant transfers.Overcrowding Malus: Passengers act to avoid stations that are already near capacity.$h(n)$ (Heuristic):Euclidean distance to the nearest station of the target type.Wait Time Logic:If a train is approaching Station A, the cost of the edge leaving Station A is reduced. This dynamic weighting means a passenger might choose a longer route (distance-wise) if the train is arriving right now, rather than waiting for a shorter route with no train in sight.5.3 The Seat Reservation SystemA naive simulation causes the "thundering herd" problem, where 20 passengers wait for a train that only holds 6. Mini Metro implements a reservation system to solve this.The Reservation Algorithm:Route Decision: Passenger selects a path that involves boarding Train $T$ on Line $L$.Attempt Reservation: The passenger calls Train.ReserveSeat().The Train checks its FutureCapacity (Current Passengers + Reserved Seats).If FutureCapacity < MaxCapacity, the reservation is accepted.If FutureCapacity >= MaxCapacity, the reservation is denied.Fallback: If denied, the passenger must wait for the next train in the schedule ($T+1$) or re-evaluate the path if the wait is too long.Visualization: This is why passengers queue in an orderly fashion. They are effectively in a FIFO queue for specific train slots.6. Train and Line MechanicsThe vehicles in Mini Metro simulate momentum and capacity, acting as the dynamic agents that service the graph.6.1 Vehicle DynamicsTrains are not simple sprites moving at constant speed. They simulate basic physics.State Machine: Stopped $\to$ Accelerating $\to$ Coasting $\to$ Decelerating.Movement: Position is calculated by interpolating along the spline.Let $t$ be the normalized distance along the line segment.Position $P(t) = \text{Spline}(t)$.Speed Variations:Melbourne Trams: Travel slower than standard trains.Osaka Shinkansen: Travel significantly faster and have different acceleration curves.6.2 Dwell Time and Audio SyncWhen a train stops at a station, it enters the Boarding state.Base Dwell: A minimum stop time.Per-Passenger Dwell: Loading/Unloading a passenger takes a specific amount of time.Audio Coupling: Research indicates that the speed of embark/disembark is controlled by the audio system. The "ticks" of passengers boarding are synchronized to the beat of the soundtrack.6.3 Capacity and AssetsLocomotive Capacity:Standard: 6 passengers.Cairo/Mumbai: 4 passengers.Carriages:A Carriage is a modifier asset attached to a Locomotive.Effect: Adds +6 capacity.Rendering: Carriages trail the locomotive physically on the spline.Packing Algorithm: Passengers inside the train are visualized as small geometric shapes. These shapes are likely packed using a simple grid or circle-packing algorithm within the train's bounding box to maximize visual clarity.7. The Audio-Visual PipelineMini Metro's aesthetic is not just a style; it is a technical constraint that dictates the rendering pipeline. The game uses no sprites for its primary elements; everything is procedurally generated geometry.7.1 Line Smoothing (Catmull-Rom Splines)To emulate the "Harry Beck" tube map style, lines must be smooth but constrained. The game uses Centripetal Catmull-Rom Splines.Why Catmull-Rom?Unlike Bezier curves, a Catmull-Rom spline is guaranteed to pass through its control points (the stations). This is essential for a game where the connection points are functional nodes. The "Centripetal" variant is chosen because it avoids the loops and cusps (self-intersections) that can occur with Uniform splines when control points are irregularly spaced.Spline Algorithm Implementation:Input: A list of station points $P_0, P_1, \dots, P_n$.Control Points: For a segment between $P_i$ and $P_{i+1}$, the algorithm requires four points: $P_{i-1}, P_i, P_{i+1}, P_{i+2}$.Endpoint Handling: For the first and last segments, the algorithm extrapolates "virtual" control points to maintain curvature.Mesh Generation:The spline defines the central path.The renderer generates a mesh strip (ribbon) by calculating the normal vector at each step along the spline and offsetting vertices by $\pm \text{LineWidth}/2$.Joints: Vertices at sharp turns use "Round Joins" to prevent visual tearing.7.2 Junction Handling and Parallel LinesWhen multiple lines share a track segment (e.g., Red and Blue lines both going from A to B), they do not overlap. They act as Parallel Offsets.Logic: The "Line Manager" detects shared edges.Offset Calculation:Calculate the normal vector perpendicular to the segment direction.Shift Line A by $+Offset$, Line B by $-Offset$.The offset distance is dynamic based on the number of concurrent lines.7.3 Procedural Audio (Total Serialism)The audio is generated using a technique called "Total Serialism," where game data maps directly to musical parameters.Table 2: Data-to-Audio MappingGame ParameterAudio ParameterNumber of Stations on LineSequence Length (Rhythm)Order of StationsSequence OrderStation TypeTimbre (Instrument)Station OccupancyDynamics (Volume)Screen PositionPanning (Stereo Field)Line SpeedPulse Attack TimeImplementation Directive:The audio engine must query the Line object every measure. It constructs a melody where each note corresponds to a station. A loop with 4 stations creates a 4-step sequence. As the player adds stations, the time signature and melody evolve procedurally.8. ConclusionThe reconstruction of Mini Metro is an exercise in balancing algorithmic rigidity with visual fluidity. The game mechanics are not driven by complex AI, but by simple agents reacting to a user-defined graph topology.
